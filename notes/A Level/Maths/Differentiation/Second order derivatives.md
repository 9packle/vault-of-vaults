Differentiating $y$ with respect to $x$ gives us $\frac{dy}{dx}$, and differentiating that then gives us 

$$
\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}
$$

This is the rate of the change of $\frac{dy}{dx}$ with respect to $x$, or the rate of change of the gradient with respect to $x$.

In function notation it is written as 

$$
f''(x)
$$

Second order derivatives can be used to find and differentiate between maximum and minimum points. At a maximum point $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ and $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0$, and at a minimum point $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ and $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0$

At a point of inflection $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$, as both the gradient and the rate of change of the gradient with respect to $x$ equal $0$.


They can also be used to find if a curve is convex or concave. If $f''(x) < 0$ for all values of $x$, then the curve $f(x)$ is concave, and if $f''(x) > 0$ for all $x$, then $f(x)$ is convex.
