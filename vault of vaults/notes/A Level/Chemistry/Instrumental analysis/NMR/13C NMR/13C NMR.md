Only around 1% of the carbon atoms are $^{13}\textup{C}$, meaning that there won't be any meaningful peak splitting in a $^{13}\textup{C}$ NMR since the chance of an adjacent carbon being a $^{13}\textup{C}$ is very low.

The number of peaks in a spectrum show the number of different carbon environments:

Here there are 5 peaks which shows that there are 5 different carbon environments in 1-methylethyl propanoate:

![[Screenshot 2026-05-23 114010.png|422]]

Although there are 6 different carbons, there are only 5 environments since the $\textup{CH}_3$ groups bonded to the $\textup{CH}$ are in identical environments.

For the horizontal scale $\delta$, it is based off of TMS or tetramethylsilane which produces a peak at 0 $\delta$ ppm. It is defined in this way since all 4 $\textup{CH}_3$ groups are in the same environment and are all bonded in the same way to the $\textup{Si}$ atom. In addition the electrons in the $\textup{C} - \textup{Si}$ bond are much closer to the carbon, meaning that the carbon nuclei are the most shielded from external magnetic field, making it appear to the right of almost every other molecule on a scale.

The scale of $\delta$ ppm indicates how much less strong the magnetic field needed is to produce resonance in a molecule compared to TMS - e.g. a shift of 67 means it requires 67 millionths less than the field needed by TMS to produce resonance.

Substances are also usually measured in solution, with the solvent being $\textup{CDCl}_3$ which produces an easily recognisable peak, making it easy to remove from the final NMR spectrum.
