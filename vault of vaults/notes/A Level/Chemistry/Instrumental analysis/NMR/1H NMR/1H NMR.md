Since $^1\textup{H}$ is the most common isotope of hydrogen, there will be significant peak splitting in $^1\textup{H}$ NMR spectra. This can also give us more information on the molecule and allow us to find its full structure.

[[Interpreting 1H NMR]]

The number of peaks in a spectrum show the number of different hydrogen environments:

![[Screenshot 2026-05-23 123456.png]]

Here there are two peaks which shows that there are two carbon environments.

The ratio of areas under the peaks, or the integrals, can tell us the ratio of hydrogen atoms in those environments - in this case the ratio is 3:1.

For the horizontal scale $\delta$, it is based off of TMS or tetramethylsilane which produces a peak at 0 $\delta$ ppm. It is defined in this way since all 12 $\textup{H}$ atoms are in the same environment and are all bonded in the same way to the $\textup{Si}$ atom. In addition the electrons in the $\textup{C} - \textup{Si}$ bond are much closer to the hydrogen than in most other compounds, meaning that the hydrogen nuclei are the most shielded from external magnetic field, making it appear to the right of almost every other molecule on a scale.

The scale of $\delta$ ppm indicates how much less strong the magnetic field needed is to produce resonance in a molecule compared to TMS - e.g. a shift of 67 means it requires 67 millionths less than the field needed by TMS to produce resonance.

Substances are also usually measured in solution, with the solvent being $\textup{CDCl}_3$ which does not appear in a $^1\textup{H}$ HMR since deuterium has an even number of protons and neutrons in its nucleus.
