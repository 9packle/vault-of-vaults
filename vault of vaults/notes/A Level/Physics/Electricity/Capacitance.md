Capacitance is the charge per unit of potential difference across the plates of a capacitor, or as an equation:
$$
C = \frac{Q}{V}
$$
The capacitance of a vacuum-spaced, parallel plate capacitor is given by
$$
C = \frac{\epsilon_0A}{d}
$$
A capacitor doesn't conduct electricity but can keep charges separate to sustain the electric field generated by a cell. The plates store equal and opposite charges on themselves to store energy.

The electrical potential energy stored by a capacitor is given by
$$
U = \frac{1}{2}QV = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C}
$$
For capacitors in series:
$$
C = (\frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} + \ldots)^{-1}
$$
For capacitors in parallel:
$$
C = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 + \ldots
$$
When a capacitor is charged, its charge varies with time like so:
$$
Q = Q_0(1 - e^{-\frac{t}{RC}})
$$
and when discharging:
$$
Q = Q_0e^{-\frac{t}{RC}}
$$
These equations can be obtained through integration.
