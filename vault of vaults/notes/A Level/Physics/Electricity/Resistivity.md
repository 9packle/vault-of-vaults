Resistivity is an intrinsic quantity of a material, meaning that it won't change with the volume of the material. The equation for resistivity is
$$
\rho = \frac{RA}{l}
$$
where $\rho$ is the resistivity, $R$ is the resistance, $A$ is the cross-sectional area of the wire and $l$ is the length of the wire.

For most metals, its resistivity increases almost linearly as the temperature rises from room temperature:
$$
\rho_T = \rho_0(1+\alpha(T - T_0))
$$
$\rho_T$ is the resistivity at temperature $T$, $\rho_0$ is the temperature at a reference temperature of $T_0$ and $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity.
